Uber accelerate

The ride-hailing app’s timetable for an IPO is slow

Uber’s new chief executive says the company might go public between 2019 and 2021. He should go sooner. In Dara Khosrowshahi’s first meeting with employees on Wednesday he told them that — although public life “sucks” because of investors’ short-term focus — the ride-hailing app would eventually do an initial public offering.

In reality, Uber has helped repudiate the idea that venture-backed companies should defer IPOs indefinitely. Employees have been left demoralised — or just left — as Uber’s sexual harassment scandal spiralled out of control. An ineffective board failed to impose discipline on executives and then indulged in damaging lawsuits.

Not every public company imposes strong governance but it is hard to imagine that a traditional shareholder base would have allowed the situation to fester.

Public enemies aver that Uber’s share price would have fallen on the scandals. But this has happened anyway. The privilege of privacy has been diluted, thanks to the mutual funds who piled into unicorn investing and are obliged to mark their investments up or down on a quarterly basis. In Uber’s case, Vanguard and Hartford reduced their valuations by 15 per cent at the end of June.

Of course, the main reason to wait would be that Uber cannot demonstrate that it has a sustainable business on a path to profitability. If that were the case it should worry Mr Khosrowshahi, who has just swapped $150m of Expedia options for an undisclosed slice of Uber.

Critics contend that Uber’s business model is fundamentally flawed. The surprising fightback from Lyft, a local competitor who has taken recent share from Uber, shows that global monopoly is not straightforward.

From what is visible from the company’s skimpy financial releases, however, Uber is on course to make about $8bn a year in “adjusted” revenues, net of the portion paid to the drivers, and heavy, though diminishing, losses.

If Uber is viewed as a typical internet company, those revenues are more than adequate to support its current $70bn valuation. Snap, the lossmaking messaging app that has arguably fiercer competition than Uber, went public this year at a $20bn valuation on just $400m of annual revenues.

Employees and current investors would gain liquidity. Public shareholders can, in fact, be perfectly patient — just ask Elon Musk. Privacy is not a shield but a burden for Uber.
